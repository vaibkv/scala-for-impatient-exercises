                                                            Daniel Westheide Mod Den • 5 years ago

                                                            Den, thanks for the kind words! As for your question, of course, general statements are difficult, and as I said, it's often application-specific how to use and configure ExecutionContexts for different parts of your application.

                                                            However, I would argue that in most cases, executing CPU-intensive tasks in the web threads is not a good idea. As you said, the web server would only be able to reject connections. If you have a dedicated execution context for certain CPU-intensive parts of your application, you will at least be able to respond with a 503 Service Unavailable immediately, because your web server thread pool is not exhausted. Also, you may have parts certain functionality that is not CPU-intensive, and this functionality would still be available to web clients even if the CPU-intensive thread pool is currently exhausted and at its limits.

                                                            I recommend to read about bulkheads and other patterns in Michael T. Nygard's excellent book "Release It!" – separate execution contexts to keep different parts of your application isolated is one way to implement bulkheads.

                                                                                                Daniel Westheide Mod Valentin Tihomirov • 5 years ago

                                                                                                Dear Valentin,

                                                                                                if you don't agree with something or you don't understand something, there is absolutely no need at all to get rude by calling it nonsense, if you are really interested in getting an answer.

                                                                                                To keep it short:

                                                                                                1.) Passing a computation to Future#apply means that internally, a Promise is created and completed, using the implicit ExecutionContext. You use Future#apply to execute a blocking computation asynchronously, probably on a different thread. On the other hand, if you are implementing some NIO that wants to provide an Future interface to clients, you will likely work with the Promise directly and complete it yourself.

                                                                                                2.) If you have a single thread pool, you run the risk that all of them will be busy performing the long-running computations, and none will be available to process other requests. That's why you try to isolate the various parts of your application by giving them separate execution contexts/thread pools. Read about bulkheads to learn more about this.
